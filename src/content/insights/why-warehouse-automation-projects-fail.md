Most warehouse automation projects fail before the system ever goes live, because the business case is built on unstable utilization assumptions, incomplete labor-removal logic, and weak workflow underwriting.

## Where failure starts
- project underwriting
- incorrect demand assumptions
- overstated labor savings
- ignored workflow constraints

## The core economic failure
Automation economics hold only if realized utilization remains above the payback floor set during underwriting. When utilization drops below that floor, fixed-capacity infrastructure cannot recover its capital and support costs on the operating timeline expected.

```text
U ≥ U_min
```

Failure occurs when realized utilization falls below the payback threshold.

## Common failure modes
### Demand shortfall
Forecast volume does not materialize, account mix changes, or peak assumptions are treated as baseline conditions. Installed capacity then runs below economic viability.

### Downstream bottlenecks
Upstream automation accelerates one node while packing, sortation, or outbound clearing remains constrained. Throughput does not convert into shipped output.

### SKU velocity mismatch
Systems are modeled on concentrated movement patterns while live SKU demand is long-tail and uneven, reducing task density and effective utilization.

### Integration instability
WMS, control-layer, and exception-flow integration introduce delays and reliability issues that compress realized throughput and extend ramp periods.

### Retrofit conflicts
Legacy building geometry, circulation limits, and staging constraints reduce real system performance versus modeled assumptions.

## What to test before approval
1. peak-to-average ratio
2. removable labor share
3. downstream capacity
4. client concentration risk
5. integration cost
6. architecture rigidity

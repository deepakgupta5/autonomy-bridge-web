A robotics pilot can prove that a machine performs a task. It does not prove that the deployment will scale economically across real workflows, real utilization patterns, and real integration conditions.

## What pilots prove, and what they do not
<div class="grid grid-cols-1 md:grid-cols-2 gap-6">
  <section class="rounded-lg border border-neutral-200 p-5">
    <h3>Technical feasibility</h3>
    <ul class="list-disc pl-5 space-y-2">
      <li>task performance in controlled conditions</li>
      <li>local throughput improvement potential</li>
      <li>basic reliability and operator usability</li>
      <li>limited interface validation</li>
    </ul>
  </section>
  <section class="rounded-lg border border-neutral-200 p-5">
    <h3>Scale viability</h3>
    <ul class="list-disc pl-5 space-y-2">
      <li>sustained utilization across demand cycles</li>
      <li>real labor removal converted into savings</li>
      <li>end-to-end workflow integration under load</li>
      <li>replicable economics across rollout sites</li>
    </ul>
  </section>
</div>

## The four structural gaps
- pilot density vs live demand variability
- gross productivity vs removable labor
- local success vs full-workflow integration
- single-site success vs multi-site rollout cost

## Why replication gets harder
### Integration costs scale nonlinearly
Each additional site introduces unique interfaces, exception logic, and support overhead, so rollout cost and effort increase faster than pilot assumptions suggest.

### Site conditions differ
Building geometry, inventory profile, shift design, and dock flow vary by location, reducing direct portability of pilot outcomes.

### Workflow variance compounds
As deployment scope expands, variance in SKU behavior, order mix, and labor practices compounds and weakens standardized performance expectations.

### Rollout friction extends timelines
Commissioning windows, retraining requirements, and staged cutovers slow ramp speed, delaying utilization stabilization and extending payback timelines.
